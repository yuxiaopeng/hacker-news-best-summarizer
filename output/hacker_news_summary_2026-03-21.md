## 3. arXiv 宣布从康奈尔独立

**原文标题**: ArXiv declares independence from Cornell

**原文链接**: [https://www.science.org/content/article/arxiv-pioneering-preprint-server-declares-independence-cornell](https://www.science.org/content/article/arxiv-pioneering-preprint-server-declares-independence-cornell)

ArXiv，这个彻底改变了科学出版的开创性预印本服务器，在历经三十余载后，宣布脱离康奈尔大学独立。该平台拥有近250万篇涵盖各种科学学科的论文，现已正式转型，成为由ArXiv社区及其全球可持续发展联盟管理的独立实体。

此举旨在巩固ArXiv的长期可持续性和治理，确保其继续作为全球研究人员的免费开放获取资源运行。虽然康奈尔大学将在过渡期内继续提供托管基础设施和一些运营支持，但战略方向、资金筹措和发展的责任将由一个新的治理结构承担，该结构包括ArXiv社区咨询委员会和成员代表。

此次转型之际，ArXiv的运营成本因投稿量增加和对现代化基础设施的需求而不断增长。新结构旨在使资金来源多样化，摆脱以往对资助和成员机构的依赖，可能通过更广泛的联盟模式来实现。此次独立是该平台迈出的重要一步，体现了科学界致力于将ArXiv维护为一个重要的公共产品，使其免受单一机构的直接行政监督。

Windows Insider计划团队的帕万·达武鲁里（Pavan Davuluri）概述了微软在大量社区反馈推动下，对Windows质量的再度承诺。本月和下月将预览的即时更改包括：更多任务栏自定义选项（垂直/顶部位置）、通过减少不必要的Copilot入口点实现有目的的AI集成，以及通过更大的用户控制权（跳过、暂停、减少重启）来减少Windows更新带来的干扰。其他改进旨在实现更快的“文件资源管理器”、对小组件的更多控制、更简洁且渠道更清晰的Windows Insider计划，以及改进的“反馈中心”。

## 9. A Japanese glossary of chopsticks faux pas (2022)

**原文标题**: A Japanese glossary of chopsticks faux pas (2022)

**原文链接**: [https://www.nippon.com/en/japan-data/h01362/](https://www.nippon.com/en/japan-data/h01362/)

The article, "A Japanese Glossary of Chopsticks Faux Pas," provides an overview of *kiraibashi*—various inappropriate ways of using chopsticks that go against dining etiquette in Japan. The glossary lists numerous actions, ranging from bad manners to serious taboos, each explained with its Japanese name.

Two specific practices are highlighted as grave taboos due to their association with funeral rites. *Awasebashi* (passing food from one pair of chopsticks to another) is forbidden as it mirrors the post-cremation custom of transferring bone fragments. Equally serious is *Tatebashi* (standing chopsticks upright in a bowl of rice), which resembles a Buddhist funeral offering.

Other common faux pas include issues of hygiene and respect, such as *Araibashi* (cleaning chopsticks in soup), *Neburibashi* (licking chopsticks), and *Ogamibashi* (holding chopsticks between both hands while giving thanks). Actions like *Madoibashi* (hovering chopsticks indecisively over dishes), *Yosebashi* (pulling a dish with chopsticks), *Sashibashi* (pointing with chopsticks or spearing food), and *Tatakibashi* (tapping chopsticks on a dish) are also considered rude. The article emphasizes that understanding and avoiding these *kiraibashi* are essential for proper dining etiquette and showing respect in Japanese culture.

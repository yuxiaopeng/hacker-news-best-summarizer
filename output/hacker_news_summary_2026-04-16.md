## 1. Google broke its promise to me – now ICE has my data

**原文标题**: Google broke its promise to me – now ICE has my data

**原文链接**: [https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/04/google-broke-its-promise-me-now-ice-has-my-data](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/04/google-broke-its-promise-me-now-ice-has-my-data)

In September 2024, Ph.D. candidate Amandla Thomas-Johnson, studying in the U.S. on a student visa, briefly attended a pro-Palestinian protest. In April 2025, Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) sent Google an administrative subpoena requesting his data. The following month, Google disclosed his information to ICE without notifying him, breaking a nearly decade-long promise to alert users before handing their data to law enforcement.

Google's policy includes exceptions like court-issued gag orders, none of which applied to Thomas-Johnson's case despite ICE "requesting" no notification. Thomas-Johnson learned of the disclosure only *after* Google had already shared his data, via an email stating the process was "final." This contrasted with an associate who received advance notice, allowing their subpoena to be withdrawn.

Thomas-Johnson, who had left the U.S. due to federal scrutiny, believed he was out of reach. His lawyer at the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) obtained the subpoena, which requested subscriber information like IP addresses, physical address, and session times, forming a detailed surveillance profile. Thomas-Johnson now faces intimidation, fear of heightened scrutiny, and travel concerns.

The EFF has filed complaints with the California and New York Attorneys General, accusing Google of deceptive trade practices for failing to uphold its notification policy. The incident underscores how law enforcement can combine state power with corporate data, facilitating investigations that can impact individuals engaged in protected political speech.

## 10. 想写编译器？只需读这两篇论文 (2008)

**原文标题**: Want to write a compiler? Just read these two papers (2008)

**原文链接**: [https://prog21.dadgum.com/30.html](https://prog21.dadgum.com/30.html)

The article criticizes traditional compiler books for being overly complex and broad, making it difficult for aspiring compiler writers to get started. It proposes a more accessible path using two key resources.

First, Jack Crenshaw's 1988 series, "Let's Build a Compiler!", is highly recommended for its clear, beginner-friendly approach. It simplifies compiler construction, focusing on single-pass compilers with intermingled parsing and code generation, and basic optimizations. Originally in Pascal, C and Forth versions exist. Crenshaw's series, however, omits an internal program representation like an Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) for simplicity, which is easier to handle in higher-level languages.

To address this omission and provide a modern perspective, the second recommendation is "A Nanopass Framework for Compiler Education" by Sarkar, Waddell, and Dybvig. This paper posits that a compiler is a series of simple transformations on a program's internal representation. It advocates for using numerous "nanopasses," each performing a minimal, distinct transformation. Written in Scheme, it offers a structured framework for managing these passes.

By combining Crenshaw's practical, direct approach with the Nanopass framework's emphasis on structured internal representations and simple transformations, the author suggests beginners can effectively build compilers without being initially overwhelmed by comprehensive academic texts like the "Dragon Book."
